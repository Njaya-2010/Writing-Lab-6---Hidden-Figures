Meet the Hidden Figures! ✨

Three incredible Black women worked as mathematicians at NASA in the 1950s and 60s. They helped send the first Americans into space — even though they faced unfair treatment because of their race. Their math skills and hard work changed history. Click each person to learn more!

👩🏾‍💼

Katherine Johnson

Mathematician • "The Human Computer"

"In math, you're either right or you're wrong." — Katherine Johnson
🔢 Katherine's calculations helped astronaut John Glenn orbit Earth safely in 1962. He refused to fly until she personally checked the numbers!
👩🏾‍💻

Dorothy Vaughan

Supervisor • Pioneer Programmer

Dorothy worked hard to get promotions — and when she finally got one, she made sure her whole team rose with her.
💻 Dorothy taught herself and her team to program NASA's new IBM computers before anyone else. She became one of the first computer programmers at NASA.
👩🏾‍🚀

Mary Jackson

Engineer • Trailblazer

Mary didn't take "no" for an answer. She petitioned the city of Hampton just to take engineering classes — and she won.
🛸 Mary became NASA's first Black female engineer. She had to get special permission from a judge just to attend night school classes that were whites-only at the time!
🌍

Why Does This Story Matter?

In the 1950s–1960s, laws called Jim Crow laws kept Black and white people separated in many places. Katherine, Dorothy, and Mary had to use separate bathrooms, eat in separate cafeterias, and weren't always treated fairly at work. Despite all that, they solved math problems that no one else could. They proved that talent and hard work can break down unfair barriers. Their story was "hidden" for a long time — but now the world knows their names! 🌟
